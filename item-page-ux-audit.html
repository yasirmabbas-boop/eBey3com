🔍 Issues by Severity
10 issues identified — all are layout/information-architecture problems, not missing data
Critical

1. Action Buttons Buried Below 5 Sections

Buy Now / Add to Cart / Bid / Make Offer buttons are at position #7 — below images, title, seller, price, shipping meta, AND return policy. On mobile that's 2-3 full scrolls down.
Move action buttons directly below the price section (position #5). eBay places "Buy It Now" and "Add to Cart" immediately under the price, before any metadata.
eBay pattern: Image → Title → Price → [Buy Now / Add to Cart] → everything else. The action is always within the first viewport. Your current layout forces users to scroll past information they don't need yet before they can act.
Lines 968-1293 in product.tsx — entire action block
Critical

2. Price Separated from Its Context

Price sits at position #4 but shipping cost is at position #5 in a separate meta section. Users can't calculate total cost at a glance. For auctions, the bid count is small gray text under the price, and the countdown is in its own orange box below.
Group price + shipping + (for auctions: bid count + countdown) into a single unified price block. Show the total cost or "Price + X shipping" in one visual unit. eBay shows "Price + Free Shipping" or "Price + $X.XX shipping" as a single reading unit.
eBay pattern: The price, shipping, and any time urgency form one cognitive unit. Splitting them across different sections forces the user to mentally reassemble the cost, which adds friction to the EVALUATE stage.
Lines 842-878 (price) vs 882-940 (shipping meta)
Critical

3. No Sticky Action Bar on Mobile

When scrolling through description, specs, or comments, the Buy/Bid buttons are completely off-screen. There's no way to act without scrolling back up.
Add a sticky bottom bar on mobile with the price + primary CTA button (Buy Now or Place Bid). eBay, Amazon, and every major marketplace has this. The bar should appear once the user scrolls past the main action buttons.
eBay pattern: A persistent bottom bar with "[Price] — Buy It Now" ensures the user can act at ANY point during their evaluation. Without this, users who read the description or comments hit a dead-end — they need to scroll back up, and some won't.
High

4. Trust Signals After the Point of Action

"Cash on Delivery" and "Buyer Protection" callout boxes appear at position #8 — AFTER the Buy button. Users must already decide to buy before they see the trust reassurances.
Move COD and Buyer Protection badges ABOVE the action buttons, as compact trust icons (not full callout boxes). They should reassure the user BEFORE the decision point. eBay places "Money Back Guarantee" above the action buttons.
Trust signals are decision enablers. Placing them after the action buttons means they're only seen by users who already scrolled past the buy option — the exact users who might have hesitated and need reassurance.
Lines 1383-1398 — COD and buyer protection callouts
High

5. Share Buttons Take Prime Real Estate

5 large social share buttons (WhatsApp, Facebook, Twitter, Telegram, Copy Link) sit right after the action buttons, each taking full-width flex space. Plus the Instagram Share Card component above them.
Collapse shares into a single "Share" icon/button in the top toolbar (near the favorite button), or a small row of compact icons AFTER description — not in the action zone. eBay uses a single share icon at the top of the page.
Social sharing is a secondary action used by <5% of users. Giving it the same visual weight as Buy/Cart pushes everything below it further down and dilutes the primary conversion funnel.
Lines 1296-1366 — share buttons block + Instagram share card
High

6. Return Policy Interrupts the Buying Flow

A full amber callout box for return policy sits between shipping meta and action buttons (position #6). It's a prominent visual block that breaks the user's momentum from evaluating price to acting.
Move return policy to a compact one-line row inside the shipping/meta section (e.g., "Returns: 14 days"). The detailed policy can expand on tap or live below description. eBay shows returns as a single line: "Returns: 30 day returns. Buyer pays return shipping."
Return policy is important but it's a reassurance detail, not evaluation data. Giving it its own prominent callout block right before the buy button creates visual weight that slows momentum.
Lines 942-958 — return policy section
High

7. Quantity Selector Placement (Fixed-Price)

The quantity selector appears inside the action buttons area, with ± buttons and counter. But it only shows when remaining quantity > 1, which means the layout shifts depending on stock — inconsistent experience.
Move quantity selector into the price section as a row: "Quantity: [−] 1 [+] • X available". This gives users the full cost picture (price × quantity + shipping) in one place before they hit Buy.
Lines 1199-1208
Medium

8. Specs Section at the Very Bottom

Brand, category, and detailed specifications are at position #11 — the very last thing on the page. Users looking for specific details (shoe size, material, color) have to scroll past everything.
Move key specs (brand, condition, size, color — the purchase-critical ones) into a compact row right below the title, similar to how eBay shows "Condition: New with tags" prominently near the top. Full specs table stays at the bottom for completeness.
eBay surfaces "Item specifics" (condition, brand, size, model) in a highlighted box near the top. These are evaluation data — they should be easy to scan without scrolling to the bottom.
Lines 1434-1470
Medium

9. Auction Countdown Visually Disconnected

For auctions, the countdown timer is inside an orange box with its own border and background, separate from the bid price above it. The WS connection status and "last update" text add clutter.
Merge the countdown into the price block: "25,000 د.ع (current bid) · 23 bids · ends in 2h 15m". The urgency should be felt AS the user reads the price, not as a separate callout. Hide WS debug info from users.
Lines 851-869
Medium

10. No "Similar Items" or "More from Seller"

The page has a SIMILAR_PRODUCTS mock array defined (line 54-62) but it's never rendered. There's no cross-sell or "keep browsing" section at the bottom. Once users finish reading, it's a dead-end.
Add a "More from this seller" horizontal scroll + "Similar items" grid below comments/specs. This keeps users engaged with the platform even if they don't buy this specific item. eBay has both.
Lines 54-62 — unused SIMILAR_PRODUCTS constant
